<p><p>A hybrid vehicle refers to a vehicle that combines two or more power sources, typically an internal combustion engine and an electric motor, to propel the vehicle. Factors contributing to this growth include increasing environmental concerns, the need to reduce greenhouse gas emissions, and government initiatives supporting the adoption of cleaner technologies. Additionally, advancements in battery technology and electric components have made hybrid vehicles more reliable, efficient, and affordable.</p><p>The market has also witnessed various trends in recent times. One major trend is the growing popularity of plug-in hybrid electric vehicles (PHEVs), which allow users to charge the vehicle's battery from an external power source. Another trend is the integration of artificial intelligence and advanced driver-assistance systems in hybrid vehicles, offering features like predictive energy management and improved powertrain control.</p><p>Furthermore, the rising demand for hybrid vehicles is not only limited to passenger cars but also extends to commercial vehicles, such as trucks and buses. <p><ul><li>Parallel Hybrid Vehicle</li><li>Series Hybrid Vehicle</li></ul></p>
<p><p>The hybrid vehicle market consists of two main types: parallel hybrid vehicles and series hybrid vehicles. In a parallel hybrid vehicle, both the internal combustion engine (ICE) and the electric motor can power the vehicle simultaneously or independently, providing flexibility and higher efficiency. On the other hand, in a series hybrid vehicle, the electric motor solely powers the vehicle, while the ICE acts as a generator to recharge the battery or to provide additional power when needed. Each type has its advantages and caters to different consumer needs and preferences.</p></p>
